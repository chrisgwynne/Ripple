# Ripple — Final Business Economy Calibration Gate

The current business closure rate improved from 66.7% to 53.7%, but this remains structurally unacceptable.

Do not proceed to 1,000-resident scaling until the existing economy can sustain a stable small town.

The objective is not to suppress closures. The objective is to correct the underlying unit economics.

## Required target

For established businesses in a stable economy:

- one-year closure rate: 2–10%
- startup closure rate: may be higher, but should remain below 20–25%
- visibly pressured businesses at any time: 5–15%
- genuine recoveries must occur
- some businesses must expand
- median lifespan must be measured in years

## Audit unit economics

For every sector, report:

- average daily customers
- average spend per customer
- revenue
- wages
- rent
- utilities
- supplier costs
- tax
- owner drawings
- cash reserves
- reserve runway
- break-even customer count
- achieved customer count
- profit margin
- closure reason

Identify exactly why each failed business failed.

## Demand realism

Demand must derive from:

- local population
- age profile
- household income
- work patterns
- school activity
- tourism or external trade where enabled
- district proximity
- habits
- loyalty
- price
- quality
- reputation
- opening hours
- weather
- transport

Do not distribute demand evenly.

Residents should not choose randomly from every business in town.

Use catchments and preferences.

## Business formation gate

Do not allow a new business to open unless projected demand supports it.

Require:

- viable catchment
- sufficient startup capital
- estimated break-even demand
- acceptable local competition
- staffing plan
- reserve runway

Prevent the town from spawning more businesses than residents can support.

## Staffing

New businesses should begin lean.

Typical startup:

- owner-operated
- limited opening hours
- low initial stock
- zero or one employee

Hire only after sustained demand.

Do not spawn every business at mature staffing levels.

## Cost scaling

Scale costs by:

- floor area
- opening hours
- staff count
- equipment
- sector
- location
- energy intensity
- stock volume

Do not use broad flat costs across all businesses.

## Recovery ladder

Before closure, businesses should attempt multiple responses:

1. reduce owner drawings
2. reduce stock
3. shorten hours
4. renegotiate supplier terms
5. raise prices
6. seek finance
7. owner capital injection
8. reduce staff
9. seek buyer
10. restructure or relocate

Closure should follow failed recovery, not simply elapsed losses.

## Competition

Competition must be local and differentiated.

Businesses compete based on:

- distance
- sector
- price
- quality
- loyalty
- reputation
- opening hours
- customer demographic

A pub should not compete directly with a bookshop.

Two cafés on opposite sides of town should not split every customer equally.

## External demand

For sectors that cannot survive on local residents alone, support:

- commuters
- tourists
- nearby villages
- delivery customers
- business contracts
- institutional contracts

Factories, workshops and logistics should rely on contracts, not walk-in residents.

## Validation

Run:

- 100 seeds
- 1 year
- 5 years
- 10 years

Report by sector:

- openings
- closures
- recoveries
- expansions
- lifespan
- profit distribution
- reserve runway
- staffing
- customer demand
- failure reason

Flag:

- closure rate above target
- any sector with systemic collapse
- businesses opening without viable demand
- businesses starting overstaffed
- no recovery events
- no expansion events
- median lifespan under two years
- repeated insolvency from identical causes

## Definition of done

Do not consider calibration complete until:

- business closure rates fall into believable ranges
- some businesses fail for real reasons
- most viable businesses survive
- recoveries occur
- expansions occur
- startups are riskier than established businesses
- sector outcomes differ meaningfully
- no Chronicle suppression is required to hide broken economics
